A Tribute to Fred Shuttlesworth

"You have to be prepared to die before you can begin to live."

Born on March 18, 1922, in Mount Meigs, Alabama, Fred Shuttlesworth was a Baptist minister and one of the South's most prominent Civil Rights leaders. He worked closesly with Dr. Martin Luther King Jr., co-founding the SCLC (Southern Christian Leadership Conference) and organizing direct-action protests in Birmingham, refusing to waver even after multiple attacks.

Timeline of Life's Events

  • March 18, 1922 - Born in Mount Meigs, Montgomery County, Alabama.
  • 1925 - Moves with his family to Birmingham, Alabama.
  • 1940 - Graduates from Rosedale High School.
  • 1941 - Marries his first wife, Ruby. The couple have four children before divorcing in 1970. Ruby dies in 1971.
  • 1942 - Works as civilian truck driver at a U.S. Army air base in Mobile during World War II.
  • 1943 - Begins preaching and attends Cedar Grove Academy Bible College in Prichard.
  • 1949 - Becomes pastor of the First Baptist Church in Selma.
  • 1951 - Graduates from Selma University.
  • 1952 - Graduates from Alabama State College.
  • 1953 - Becomes pastor of Bethel Baptist in Collegeville.
  • 1955 - Petitions the Birmingham City Commission to hire black police officers. He issues a statement endorsed by 76 ministers and leads a delegation to City Hall.

    • The Rev. Fred Shuttlesworth at Sardis Baptist Church.

  • Feb. 1956 - Shuttlesworth and lawyer Arthur Shores accompany Autherine Lucy in her attempt to become the first black student to attend the University of Alabama.
  • Jun. 1956 - Preaches at Sardis Baptist Church in Birmingham on the night he helps to start the Alabama Christian Movement for Human Rights -- a week after Alabama Attorney General John Patterson outlaws the NAACP. The church "was packed," Shuttlesworth said later. "The thing you have to remember is that I was challenging the whole segregation law. I was saying what I wanted to say, and I was screaming against segregation."
  • Dec. 25, 1956 - A Ku Klux Klan bomb explodes between the church and the parsonage where Shuttlesworth slept, collapsing the house on top of him. "He was bombed in bed," Jackson said. Shuttlesworth walks away, and preaches that it was a miracle.
  • Dec. 26, 1956 - Six days after a U.S. Supreme Court ruling took effect ordering Montgomery city buses to integrate, the Rev. Fred Shuttlesworth and others challenge the law in Birmingham by joining white passengers on a city bus.
  • Feb. 1957 - Shuttlesworth, King, the Rev. Ralph David Abernathy and other ministers found the Southern Christian Leadership Conference.

    • Shuttlesworth is stopped before entering the whites only waiting room at Birmingham's Terminal station.

  • Mar. 1957 - Lamar Weaver, an early supporter of civil rights, greets the Rev. Fred Shuttlesworth and his wife, Ruby, in the whites-only waiting room at Birmingham's train depot, Terminal Station. One day earlier, the Alabama Public Service Commission rules that the waiting rooms must remain segregated. Minutes after this photo was taken, police eject Weaver from the waiting room, and he is attacked by a mob of more than 100 white protesters. The Shuttlesworths later board a train.
  • Jun. 1958 - Bethel Baptist Church is bombed again. Petitions for the desegregation of Birmingham city schools. He also renews a lawsuit to desegregate the city's parks.
  • Oct. 1958 - At the Birmingham jail, the Rev. Fred Shuttlesworth encounters barriers as he posts bail after being arrested for sitting in the white section of a city bus.
  • 1960 - Shuttlesworth is sued along with The New York Times for libel in the landmark Sullivan v. New York Times case. Shuttlesworth is found liable and damages against him are set at $500,000. His car is confiscated by law enforcement officials.
  • May 15, 1961 - Shuttlesworth, center, and a group of Freedom Riders discuss plans at Birmingham's Greyhound Terminal after drivers refused to carry them farther. One day earlier, a bus is bombed in Anniston and passengers on a second bus are beaten in Birmingham. The Freedom Riders were student activists and other volunteers who challenged segregation on interstate buses and in bus terminals. Later, these riders caught a plane out of Birmingham to New Orleans.
  • May 18, 1961 - In a CBS documentary, newsman Howard K. Smith calls Shuttlesworth "the man most feared by Southern racists."
  • Summer 1961 - Moves to Cincinnati to become pastor of Revelation Baptist Church.

    • Good Friday, 1963 Fred Shuttlesworth, Ralph Abernathy and Martin Luther King, Jr. taking part in Civil Rights demonstrations in Birmingham. The three ministers were arrested, and it was during this incarceration that King wrote his famous "Letter From Birmingham Jail."

  • 1962 - Accompanies the Rev. Martin Luther King Jr. and others to a meeting in Washington with Attorney General Robert F. Kennedy and Vice President Lyndon Johnson, calling for a second Emancipation Proclamation to end segregation.
  • Apr. 1963 - Leads the first mass marches to Birmingham City Hall with King. Police use billy clubs against marchers.
  • May. 1963 - Shuttlesworth is knocked down by a fire hose and hospitalized.
  • Sep. 1963 - Speaks at the funeral of Carole Robertson, one of the four girls killed in the bombing of Sixteenth Street Baptist Church. King delivers the main eulogies for the other three girls killed.
  • 1964 - The U.S. Supreme Court reverses the ruling against Shuttlesworth and others in Sullivan v. New York Times, creating a landmark free speech case.
  • Jan. 1965 - King kicks off a voter registration drive in Selma. With King are the Rev. Fred Shuttlesworth, left; the Rev. Ralph Abernathy, right; and the Rev. Andrew Young, far right.
  • Feb. 1965 - Speaks at a rally in Selma with Malcolm X.
  • Mar. 7, 1965 - Selma-to-Montgomery march for voting rights is halted at the Edmund Pettus Bridge by state troopers and police using tear gas and billy clubs in what becomes known as "Bloody Sunday."
  • Mar. 9, 1965 - Shuttlesworth and others join King in prayer just across the Edmund Pettus Bridge. That night, the Rev. James Reeb, a white minister supporting civil rights, is severely beaten and dies.
  • 1966 - Starts the Greater New Light Baptist Church in Cincinnati. Later, Shuttlesworth becomes a landlord, at one point owning more than 80 rental units.
  • 1988 - Establishes the Shuttlesworth Housing Foundation, an organization that helps low-income families to buy their own homes.

    • Fred Shuttlesworth standing hand-in-hand with the statue erected in his honor.

  • 1992 - A statue of Shuttlesworth is placed in front of the Birmingham Civil Rights Institute, which he helped to create.
  • 2001 - Shuttlesworth eceives the President's Citizens Medal from President Bill Clinton.
  • 2002 - The Birmingham Civil Rights Institute creates the Fred L. Shuttlesworth Human Rights Award in 2002, honoring him as the first recipient as well as the award's namesake.
  • 2003 - Shuttlesworth is named interim president of the Southern Christian Leadership Conference and in August 2004 is voted president during a convention marred by infighting.
  • 2004 - Resigns from the SCLC, complaining that "deceit, mistrust and a lack of spiritual discipline and truth have eaten away at the core of this once-hallowed organization."
  • 2005 - Retires as pastor of Greater New Light Baptist Church.
  • Aug. 2005 - Shuttlesworth has surgery to remove a benign brain tumor, which is diagnosed after he suffers a seizure in May.

    • President-to-be Sen. Barack Obama, second from left, looks on as civil rights leader Fred Shuttlesworth, front left, greets former President Bill Clinton in Selma, Ala., Sunday, March 4, 2007.

  • 2006 - Marries educator Sephira Ann Bailey, whom he met more than 20 years ago. She is a longtime member of Greater New Light Baptist Church and helped care for Shuttlesworth after surgery for a brain tumor. He is 84 at the time of their marriage; she is 49.
  • Mar. 2007 - Shuttlesworth and other civil rights icons are joined by, then, senator Barack Obama, former President Bill Clinton, and Hillary Clinton at the Edmund Pettus Bridge on the 42nd anniversary of "Bloody Sunday."
  • Sep. 2007 - Suffers a stroke in Cincinnati, three weeks after getting a pacemaker because of heart trouble.
  • Mar. 2008 - Returns to Birmingham to begin therapy for his stroke.
  • Jun. 2008 - Birmingham Mayor Larry Langford proposes renaming the airport for the civil right leader. The airport authority votes unanimously in July to rename it the Birmingham-Shuttlesworth International Airport.
  • Nov. 2008 - Hospitalized and bedridden following recovery from a stroke. Moved to intensive care in December to be treated for pneumonia and dehydration.
  • October 5, 2011 - Fred Shuttlesworth dies at the age of 89 at Princeton Baptist Medical Center after an extended illness.
